In the new journey, your website may be bypassed entirely. You become visible only if your product data is machine-readable and your catalog is trusted by the agent.

The signal is real and accelerating. According to a June 2025 survey of 1,000 consumers across the U.S., U.K., and Australia by Commerce and Future Commerce, 33% of Gen Z shoppers now prefer AI platforms for product research — putting them nearly at parity with the 37% who still start with a traditional search engine. Among Millennials, 26% prefer AI for product research. Adobe Analytics data, covering over one trillion visits to U.S. retail sites, found that AI-driven traffic to retail sites surged 693% year-over-year during the 2025 holiday season (November–December), with AI referrals converting 31% better than non-AI traffic sources — a meaningful quality signal, not just a volume one.

For broader context on scale: consumers still conduct far more searches on Google than on AI platforms — adoption is accelerating, but not yet dominant. The trajectory, not the current volume, is the critical variable. History suggests the window to prepare is always shorter than it appears.

Market Size Projections — Verified Primary Sources
$300B–$500B

Bain & Company (November 2025) estimates U.S. agentic commerce could reach $300–$500 billion by 2030, representing 15–25% of total U.S. online retail sales. Morgan Stanley (November 2025) projects $190–$385 billion in U.S. ecommerce spending by 2030 (10–20% market share). McKinsey (October 2025) projects $1 trillion in U.S. B2C and $3–$5 trillion globally. All three firms are projecting the same structural shift with different scope definitions.

The core of the thesis: brands will still need websites for story, inspiration, and legitimacy. But the transaction layer is migrating. Checkout friction is moving upstream into conversational interfaces. Discovery is shifting into LLM dialogue, not product listing pages. The question of "who shows up" will increasingly be answered not by a search algorithm but by a language model reasoning over structured product data.

Where this thesis is most grounded: commodity purchasing, replenishment cycles, categories with standardized technical specifications, and B2B procurement. Where it is more contested: high-consideration, emotionally charged, or fit-dependent purchases. That spectrum matters enormously for how any given retailer should respond — and we map it in detail in Section 03.

02

The Competing Theories — A Multi-Voice Analysis

This is not a settled debate. The world's sharpest observers disagree significantly on pace, depth, and shape. Understanding all sides is essential before committing strategy.

📈 The Optimist's View — Criteo CEO, Bain

"Additive, Not Replacing"

Just as ecommerce didn't kill physical stores, agentic commerce will add a new high-converting discovery layer. The total addressable market grows. Retailers who adapt gain a new channel.

⚠️ The Pessimist's View

"It's a margin problem, not a marketing problem"

The agent owns the customer relationship. Retailers become commodity fulfillment. Brand equity counts for nothing when a machine optimizes purely on price, speed, and reviews.

🎯 The Pragmatist — Benedict Evans

"Which tasks get automated — that's the question"

AI transitions happen slower than hype suggests and faster than incumbents plan for. The question isn't whether AI transforms commerce — it's which specific part of the journey it claims first.

The Expansionist View

"The past 30 years of digital evolution have shown a consistent pattern: new channels expand the ecosystem and are additive, rarely replacing what came before. Agentic commerce will follow the same trajectory, adding a powerful new layer of engagement, but not a wholesale replacement for existing ones."
Michael Komasinski, CEO, Criteo — "Agentic Commerce is Emerging, Just Not the Way Most People Expect" · December 16, 2025

This school of thought — backed by Criteo, Bain, and historical channel-shift precedent — argues that agentic commerce adds a powerful new engagement layer without demolishing existing ones. Bain's November 2025 consumer research found that around half of consumers remain cautious about letting AI agents autonomously handle purchases from start to finish, particularly for higher-consideration categories. Today's LLM platforms are strongest in the research-and-comparison phase.

The Structural Replacement View

"External LLM platforms will understand customer intent better than brands do. Brands will be invited into the decision process after intent is already formed. Visibility becomes conditional, paid, and reactive. This is not a marketing problem. It is a margin problem."
Cenk Karacaev, Medium · January 2026

This camp argues prior channel shifts are insufficient analogies. Prior transitions — search to mobile to social — changed the channel but not the buyer. Agentic commerce changes the buyer from a human to a machine. BCG's December 2025 analysis warns that without intervention, retailers risk being "reduced to background utilities in agent-controlled marketplaces," noting that agents prioritize price, ratings, and delivery speed over brand familiarity or loyalty.

The Pragmatic Middle — Benedict Evans

"AI has become the new Metaverse, in that people say it and you have no idea what they mean. The most important question isn't whether AI replaces things — it's which specific tasks it automates, and where in the value chain that happens."
Benedict Evans, B2BMX 2026 Keynote · February 2026

Evans, one of the most cited independent technology analysts, frames AI in the same historical arc as mainframes, PCs, and smartphones. The platform shift is structurally real, but its specific value capture points are still being discovered. His consistent position: transitions happen slower than hype suggests, faster than incumbents plan for, and never quite in the form predicted.

The Retail Futurist View — Doug Stephens

Doug Stephens (Retail Prophet), whose strategic frameworks have influenced Walmart, Google, LVMH, and BMW, argues the real question is not whether channels die but whether retailers can articulate a reason for existing that transcends transaction. In an agent-mediated world, the retailers who survive are those who provide something an agent cannot: genuine judgment, community, trust, and human context.

The Economist's View — Democratization of Price Transparency

The Economist, October 2025

"AI search tools can rapidly process data from company websites and publicly available sources. This democratisation of information will make it very difficult for businesses to charge anything above the fairest price. Customer loyalty could shift from brands to the AI tools themselves."

The structural implication: switching costs are falling. AI tools make price comparison so frictionless that years of brand investment can be neutralized in a single conversation. Scott Galloway's complementary analysis notes that Amazon is leveraging AI and robotics to achieve substantial reductions in fulfillment time and cost — meaning the companies that capture the most agentic commerce value are those with logistics certainty, not just content quality.

Bull Case

Additive, Not Replacing

AI becomes a high-converting discovery channel. Sites persist for story and fulfillment. Retailers with rich, structured data get surfaced more — LLMs favor authority and specificity over mass-market breadth.

Bear Case

Structural Disintermediation

LLMs own the customer relationship. Retailers become fulfillment utilities. Brand equity is irrelevant to agents optimizing for price, speed, and ratings. Direct-to-brand channels eliminate multi-brand resellers.

Most Likely

Uneven, Vertical-by-Vertical

The transition plays out differently by category. Low-consideration purchases go agentic first. High-consideration, fit-dependent categories hybridize. The question is which category you're in.

The Unknown

Agent Monetization & Training Bias

The monetization model for LLM platforms in commerce is unresolved. How agents are trained, what biases exist, and whether platforms move to advertising or transaction fees all have profound strategic implications.

Four Zones on the Spectrum

  • 1
    Full Delegation — Agents Win ImmediatelyReplenishment purchases, commodity goods, standardized products. Coffee pods, cleaning supplies, bulk consumables. The agent knows your preferences, monitors inventory, and optimizes price. According to Morgan Stanley's November 2025 research, grocery and consumer packaged goods are the fastest-growing categories for agentic commerce — already driving 49% of AI-assisted purchases. This zone is moving fast.
  • 2
    Research Delegation, Human Decision — Current Sweet SpotElectronics, apparel accessories, general sporting goods. The agent does research, comparison, and shortlisting. The human makes the final call. This is where most agentic activity is clustered right now — Bain found 30–45% of U.S. consumers already use generative AI for product research and comparison in this zone.
  • 3
    Hybrid — Agent Assists, Human CommitsSpecialty equipment, running shoes, ski gear, furniture. High-consideration, fit-dependent, often emotionally significant. Agents provide extraordinary research value. But the final purchase involves personal judgment that will remain human-led for years. Specialty retailers operating in this zone have a defensible position — but only if their product data is rich enough for agents to surface the right options in the first place.
  • 4
    Human-Led, Agent-Resistant — Long-Term FortressLuxury goods, bespoke items, products requiring physical fitting. Agents may surface options, but the decision is owned by the human and often involves in-person evaluation. The store — physical or richly immersive digital — remains essential here and is not threatened in the near term.

The strategic implication: The more you can shift your catalog and customer experience toward Zones 3 and 4 — where human judgment and domain expertise are irreplaceable — the more defensible your position in an agent-mediated world. Simultaneously, Zone 2 products must be machine-readable and agent-discoverable, or you lose them to competitors who are prepared.

AEO — The New SEO

Real-world transaction data is beginning to validate the strategic case. Alhena AI, a commerce AI platform vendor, published a 329-brand dataset showing LLM and generative AI traffic converting at 2.47% — ranking fourth among all acquisition channels, above Google Ads (1.82%) and Meta Ads (0.52%). While Alhena has a commercial interest in agentic commerce adoption, the directional finding is consistent with Adobe Analytics data and BCG's engagement research, which independently show AI-referred visitors converting better and engaging more deeply than other traffic sources. Yet most ecommerce teams have zero structured strategy for getting their products recommended by AI. That gap is the opportunity right now.

The terminology has evolved rapidly but refers to the same imperative: GEO (Generative Engine Optimization), AEO (Answer Engine Optimization), and ACO (Agentic Commerce Optimization) all mean: make your product data legible to machines that reason in language, not just index by keyword. Practically: implement JSON-LD Product schema across all product pages, write descriptions that include specific measurements and use-case language, and ensure compatibility information is explicit and consistent.

The Disintermediation Cascade

The most existential risk for multi-brand retailers is not just that traffic declines. It's that the entire customer relationship migrates to a platform that extracts value from every transaction while returning progressively less of it to the retailer.

Phase 1 · NOW
Traffic & Attention Migration
Severity: Medium — accelerating rapidly
Shoppers start their journey on ChatGPT, Perplexity, or Gemini instead of Google. They may still end up on your site to buy — but you're no longer the discovery destination. Organic search traffic begins declining. BCG data shows nearly 10% of businesses have already seen a decline in organic traffic year-over-year attributable to AI search displacement. First-party behavioral data starts degrading as fewer shoppers browse your pages.

Phase 2 · 2026–2028

Google's Universal Commerce Protocol (UCP), developed with Shopify, Walmart, Target, Mastercard, Visa, and 20+ global partners, creates a common language for seamless agent-to-checkout journeys. The retailer fulfills orders — but the customer experience belongs to the AI platform.
🛒 Checkout moving off-site 💳 AI platform owns checkout data ⛔ You become fulfillment infrastructure
Phase 3 · 2028+
Relationship Migration
Severity: Existential
The customer relationship belongs entirely to the LLM platform. They have the purchase history, the preference signals, the behavioral data. Your loyalty program is irrelevant — the AI knows more about the customer than your CRM does. Re-engagement costs rise structurally and permanently. The retailer without a data moat or proprietary agent relationship is a commodity supplier. Bain warns that retailers who "fail to define an agentic commerce strategy risk ceding control over customer data, checkout and fulfillment to third-party AI platforms."
💔 Loyalty programs hollowed out 🕳 CRM data stream goes dark 📈 Re-acquisition costs rise permanently
Most retailers are entering Phase 1 right now. The window to build capabilities that prevent Phase 2 and 3 is the next 12–18 months. This is not a 5-year strategic issue. It is a present operational one.

Bain's Direct Warning (November 2025): "Multibrand retailers and marketplaces will likely face a greater disintermediation threat. Third-party AI agents increase market transparency, giving big, low-cost, high-speed players an edge. Retailers that cannot compete based on price, unique assortment, or shipping speed alone will need to develop a competitive differentiator that is attractive to agents."

The Four Toll Bridge Owners

Amazon, Google, Walmart, and the LLM platforms (OpenAI, Google, Anthropic, Perplexity) are each building from very different starting positions. The LLM platforms are building toll bridges — structures that want to own the discovery road and become the commercial destination. Bain's research (in partnership with Similarweb) found that AI now accounts for up to 25% of referral traffic for some retailers, though it represents less than 1% of total traffic overall — a signal that is small today but accelerating fast.

Amazon's strategic response — Nova Act, an AI agent for web browsing and autonomous shopping launched in early 2026 — represents its attempt to become the agent and the destination simultaneously. Most retailers have neither the scale nor the engineering resources to replicate this strategy. The question for independent retailers is not how to compete with Amazon's agent, but how to ensure their products are surfaced by every agent.

The Martech & Customer Data Reckoning

For the past decade, retail marketing ran on a proven formula: capture behavioral data, segment audiences, send personalized messages, and re-engage at scale. That entire paradigm is under structural pressure.

In the old funnel, you had data at every step — browse behavior, cart additions, abandoned sessions, and conversion signals. In the new funnel, the agent owns the top of the funnel entirely. The behavioral data that powered email retargeting, personalization, and re-engagement simply doesn't exist anymore.

McKinsey's October 2025 analysis warns that brands face potential losses of 20–50% of their traditional search channel traffic as AI-powered search displaces conventional search engines — framed as a risk scenario requiring strategic response, not an inevitable central forecast. The behavioral shift is already measurable today. Adobe's data through July 2025 showed AI-driven visits to U.S. retail sites spending 32% more time on site, viewing 10% more pages, and bouncing 27% less often than traditional traffic — a quality signal that AI-referred shoppers are further down the consideration funnel when they arrive.

The First-Party Data Imperative

A profound irony sits at the center of the agentic commerce transition: as AI agents increasingly handle the discovery phase, the behavioral data that retailers can collect from site visitors degrades — precisely when first-party data has never been more commercially valuable. Retailers who have built deep first-party data assets — purchase history, preference signals, loyalty behavior, fit data — have a structural advantage that will appreciate as the behavioral signal from standard analytics erodes.

The retailers most at risk are those who built their personalization and retargeting strategies primarily on third-party cookies and browse-behavior triggers. As agents bypass the site entirely for discovery, those signals disappear. The retailers best positioned are those who have invested in loyalty programs, preference centers, and direct customer relationships that survive independent of whether the customer visits the website.

The Emerging "Brand Twin" Concept

One of the most forward-looking concepts emerging in martech is the "Brand Twin" — an always-on, brand-owned AI agent that deeply understands individual consumers and acts on their behalf. The strategic logic is defensive as much as offensive: if you don't have an agent representing your catalog and customer relationships, a third-party LLM will represent them on terms you don't control. A brand-owned agent preserves the customer relationship even as discovery moves off-site.

Loyalty in an Agent World

There is a genuine risk, flagged by The Economist in October 2025, that customer loyalty could shift from brands to the AI tools themselves — as AI tools make comparison so frictionless that brand familiarity becomes a progressively weaker moat. What builds genuine loyalty in an agent world differs from what built it before: proprietary data the agent can use on the customer's behalf, exclusive products or access the agent cannot source elsewhere, and service quality that exceeds what commodity alternatives can offer.

Beneath the strategic narrative, a technical infrastructure is rapidly consolidating. These protocols determine which retailers are agent-accessible and which are invisible. Understanding them is no longer optional for any ecommerce leader.

The Infrastructure Moment

Shopify's Summer 2025 Edition rolled out built-in MCP support across all stores with no custom setup required. Through the MCP endpoint, an AI assistant can search the catalog, retrieve product details with real-time inventory, add items to cart, answer FAQs, and initiate checkout — all in a single conversation. For Shopify merchants, infrastructure participation is now a default capability. For retailers on other platforms, it requires deliberate engineering investment.

Google's Universal Commerce Protocol, published in January 2026, is an open-source standard providing tokenized payments, verifiable credentials, and a common language for agent-to-checkout commerce journeys. Its endorsement by Adyen, American Express, Best Buy, Flipkart, Macy's, Mastercard, Stripe, The Home Depot, Visa, Zalando, and others signals that this is becoming baseline infrastructure, not an emerging experiment.

Strategic Implication

MCP + UCP + A2A together constitute the "plumbing" of agentic commerce. Retailers need to think of API infrastructure and data quality not as engineering concerns but as commercial real estate. If your catalog doesn't have an address in this ecosystem, your products don't exist to the agents that will increasingly mediate purchase decisions.

The Vendor Landscape — Who's Building What

A generation of martech and commerce-technology vendors is being restructured around this shift. Some are pivoting successfully; others face commoditization. Understanding the landscape helps identify where to invest and where to build internally.

Catalog Enrichment & Product Intelligence

A category of vendors is building specifically on the insight that structured product data is the foundation of both search performance and LLM discoverability. These companies offer AI-powered attribute enrichment, product tagging, and data normalization — effectively translating human-written catalog descriptions into machine-readable structured data at scale. The market opportunity is significant because most retailer catalogs remain in poor shape: missing attributes, inconsistent naming conventions, empty specification fields, and marketing copy written for humans rather than machines.

The build-versus-buy question is genuine. For large catalogs (100,000+ SKUs across multiple categories), vendor solutions provide speed and scale that would be difficult to match internally. For specialty retailers with bounded catalog depth and deep domain expertise, LLM-assisted internal enrichment workflows are increasingly viable — and produce data that carries proprietary domain knowledge that no external vendor can replicate.

Behavioral Trigger & Lifecycle Marketing Platforms

The generation of platforms built on behavioral email and SMS personalization — triggered by browse behavior, cart abandonment, and purchase history — face a structural challenge as agentic commerce reduces the on-site data collection surface. When agents browse on behalf of shoppers, the behavioral signals that powered trigger-based marketing disappear. The platforms adapting most effectively are pivoting toward first-party data activation, predictive modeling on existing purchase history, and integration with LLM-native surfaces rather than relying solely on on-site behavioral signals.

Search, Discovery & AEO Intelligence

Platforms focused on internal site search and product discovery are being directly threatened as agentic interfaces increasingly handle the discovery layer before a shopper ever reaches the retailer's site. The adaptive pivot is toward AEO tooling — helping retailers understand how their products appear (or don't appear) in LLM recommendations, and building workflows to improve that visibility. Enterprise AEO tools now offer multi-LLM simulation capabilities, testing identical queries across GPT-4o, Claude, and Gemini to reveal how different AI systems retrieve and weight your data — a new form of search rank tracking built for the agent era.

09

What Multi-Brand Retailers Must Do

Multi-brand retailers — those who resell hundreds or thousands of third-party brands without owning any proprietary product — face the most acute version of the agentic commerce challenge. Understanding this specific threat is essential to building the right response.

Bain's analysis is explicit: "Multi-brand retailers and marketplaces will likely face a greater disintermediation threat. Third-party AI agents increase market transparency, giving big, low-cost, high-speed players an edge." The structural vulnerability is threefold — and stacked.

The Three Stacked Threats

Threat 1: Brand Bypass via Direct Agent Routing

When a consumer asks an agent to find the best deal on a specific branded product, the agent has no obligation to route through a multi-brand retailer. If the brand itself builds an MCP server and agentic checkout flow — an increasingly common development — an agent optimizing for price, trust, and simplicity may route directly to the brand. Multi-brand retailers must compete by offering genuine value the brand's own DTC site cannot: better price (within MAP constraints), broader compatibility context, faster shipping, or superior service reliability that agents can read and verify.

Threat 2: Catalog Data Disadvantage vs. Brands

Brands own their product data at the source. They know the exact specifications, compatibility matrices, intended use classifications, and fit guidance — information that multi-brand retailers typically receive through vendor-supplied feeds of variable quality and completeness. If agents prefer more authoritative, data-rich sources (and they demonstrably do), the retailer working from incomplete vendor feeds is structurally disadvantaged against the brand publishing primary-source data. The response: invest in first-party data enrichment that adds compatibility context, use-case guidance, and comparative intelligence that the brand itself doesn't publish.

Threat 3: Price Transparency Commoditizes Positioning

Agents make price comparison essentially frictionless and instantaneous. Any pricing advantage earned through customer loyalty, browsing habits, or low discoverability of competitors is eliminated when an agent compares every available option in seconds. If price is the primary differentiation, that advantage is structurally fragile. The response must be richer value that agents can read and cite: proprietary expertise, exclusive products, verified service quality metrics, and domain knowledge that the commodity alternative cannot provide.

The Three Strategic Opportunities

  • 1
    Become the Vertical Authority Hub No general retailer, no LLM trained on broad web data, and no individual brand can be the most authoritative structured knowledge source in a specialized vertical across all categories. A specialty retailer can. The specific knowledge — compatibility matrices, fit guides, terrain-specific recommendations, upgrade path logic, service interval data — structured as machine-readable domain expertise, becomes the kind of content LLMs cite, reference, and prefer. This is not a marketing investment. It is a data moat. The principle, documented by multiple AEO research firms, is "niche density": becoming the most authoritative source in a specific vertical outranks mass-market breadth in agent retrieval.
  • 2
    Encode Expert Judgment as Agent-Usable Data Specialty retailers possess domain expertise that general retailers and individual brands cannot replicate — staff who use the products, who understand the nuance of product selection for different users and conditions, who have accumulated years of customer fit and purchase data. In an agent-mediated world, that expertise needs to be externalized as structured data: fit recommendations mapped to user dimensions, compatibility scores, use-case-specific build recommendations, and upgrade path patterns derived from real purchase behavior. This is the kind of proprietary signal that a general LLM cannot train on unless the retailer publishes it — and publishing it creates a durable advantage.
  • 3
    Own the On-Site Agent Experience Before Off-Site Agents Do According to Constructor.com's "Preparing Retail for Agentic AI" report, 43% of shoppers want an AI-powered search bar or chatbot to explain what they need in their own words. When asked directly about a true "AI agent" on a retail site, 60% leaned positive — 23% said they would definitely try it and 37% said they probably would. Only 13% said they'd likely refuse. The customer appetite for expert AI assistance is real — and the window to own that experience on the retailer's terms is open right now. An on-site agent that knows the full catalog deeply and can guide a customer through complex selection decisions is stronger differentiation than any banner campaign — and serves as a continuous data collection mechanism for understanding catalog gaps and customer needs.

3–5 Years

Either path requires the knowledge moat built in the prior phase.

What We Know With High Confidence

  • 1
    Discovery is already shifting, and it is acceleratingThe data from Adobe, Morgan Stanley, Bain, and BCG is consistent: AI-driven traffic is growing at triple-digit or higher year-over-year rates, converting better than paid channels, and led by younger demographics who will dominate consumer spending within a decade. This is not a future trend to plan for — it is a present trend to respond to now.
  • 2
    Structured product data is now a commercial asset, not a technical detailAny retailer whose product data is not rich, consistent, and machine-readable is already losing LLM visibility to competitors who are. This is the most immediate, highest-leverage investment available. The cost of fixing it is high. The cost of not fixing it is compounding interest on a competitive debt.
  • 3
    The infrastructure layer is consolidating fastMCP is already deployed at scale on Shopify. UCP is backed by Google, Shopify, Visa, Mastercard, and 20+ global partners. These are not emerging standards debating their future — they are becoming the baseline. Retailers not integrated into this ecosystem within 18 months will face compounding disadvantage that grows harder to overcome over time.
  • 4
    First-party data becomes more valuable as behavioral signal degradesThe more commerce moves through agent interfaces, the less behavioral data retailers capture from site sessions. Retailers who have built deep first-party data assets — purchase history, preference signals, domain-specific user data — have a structural advantage that will appreciate as the standard analytics data stream thins.

What We Don't Know — The Honest Uncertainties

The pace of full transaction delegation remains genuinely uncertain for high-consideration categories. Bain's November 2025 research found around half of consumers still cautious about autonomous agent purchasing, particularly for higher-consideration items. That number will change as trust develops — but how fast, and for which categories, is not yet clear.

The monetization model for LLM platforms in commerce is unresolved. If platforms monetize through advertising (brands pay for placement), the dynamics resemble Google Shopping. If through transaction fees, they resemble Amazon Marketplace. If they maintain neutral citation, data quality wins. These different structures have profoundly different strategic implications for retailers, and we are still in the early, pre-monetization phase of most platforms' commerce ambitions.

Finally: the training biases embedded in LLMs are not transparent. An agent trained primarily on Amazon product data will have different product knowledge defaults than one trained on a broader web corpus. These biases may systematically advantage some retailers and brands over others in ways that have nothing to do with product quality or data structure — and are not yet well understood.

The Single Most Important Conclusion

Core Strategic Principle

The retailers that survive agentic commerce won't be the ones with the most SKUs, the most ad spend, or even the best prices. They will be the ones whose expertise, knowledge, and product intelligence the agent trusts — because they have encoded that intelligence as structured data, published it as authoritative content, and built the technical infrastructure for agents to access it reliably. In specialty retail, expertise has always been the moat. In agentic commerce, that expertise needs to live in a format machines can reason over. The window to build it before competitors do is closing faster than most retailers realize.

History suggests that resisting new intermediaries rarely works. Retailers who refused to list on Amazon lost reach. Those who dismissed mobile as a distraction fell behind. Agents aren't adversaries — they're channels. The sooner retailers treat them as distribution infrastructure, the sooner they can shape how their products are represented.

The website is not dead. The product page is not going away. But its role is fundamentally changing: from being the destination of the shopping journey, it is becoming the data source that feeds an ecosystem of agents, assistants, and interfaces that will surface products to buyers who may never visit it at all. Design your data strategy accordingly.
