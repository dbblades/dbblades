A structural argument is circulating among ecommerce leaders: that the website, as the primary shopping venue, is a transitional technology — and that LLMs will eventually become the interface through which most commerce flows.

In the new journey, your website may be bypassed entirely. The customer's destination is a conversation with an AI, not a visit to your site. You become visible only if your product data is machine-readable and your catalog is trusted by the agent.

The signal is real and accelerating. According to a June 2025 survey of 1,000 consumers by Commerce and Future Commerce, 33% of Gen Z consumers now prefer AI platforms for product research — putting them nearly at parity with the 37% who still start with a search engine. Adobe Analytics reported a 693% surge in AI-driven traffic to retail sites during the 2025 holiday season, with shoppers arriving from AI services converting 31% better than other traffic sources. Web traffic from AI sources on Amazon Prime Day 2025 was up 3,300% year over year.

These are not incremental numbers. They are the early signatures of a channel growing faster than any prior digital commerce transition. For framing: consumers conducted 373 times more searches on Google than ChatGPT in 2024 — a massive hill to climb, comparable to where Google itself was in 1999. The trajectory, not the present, is the thing to watch.

Market Size — Three Verified Projections
$190B–$500B

Morgan Stanley (November 2025) projects $190–$385 billion in U.S. agentic commerce by 2030 (10–20% of online retail). Bain projects $300–$500 billion (15–25%). McKinsey projects $1 trillion in U.S. B2C and $3–5 trillion globally. All three firms project the same structural shift; the range reflects differing scope definitions and adoption assumptions.

We are entering the era of Agentic Commerce, where shopping is no longer something humans do — it is something our agents do for us. In this world, the primary customer for your ecommerce store may not be a person but an autonomous software agent.

The strongest version of this thesis: brands will still need websites for story, inspiration, and legitimacy — but the transaction layer will move. Checkout friction will move upstream into conversational interfaces. Discovery will happen in LLM dialogue, not on product listing pages. The question of "who shows up" will be answered not by a search algorithm but by a language model reasoning over structured product data.

Where the thesis is most grounded: commodity and low-consideration purchasing, replenishment cycles, research-heavy categories with standardized specs, and B2B procurement. Where it is more contested: high-consideration, emotionally charged, or fit-dependent purchases. That spectrum matters enormously for how you position your business.

The Competing Theories — A Multi-Voice Analysis

This is not a settled debate. The world's sharpest observers disagree significantly on pace, depth, and shape. Understanding all sides is essential before committing strategy.

📈 The Optimist's View — Criteo CEO & Bain

"Additive, Not Replacing"

Just as ecommerce didn't kill physical stores, agentic commerce will add a new layer on top of existing commerce. Retailers who adapt get a new high-converting discovery channel. The pie gets bigger.

⚠️ The Pessimist's View

"It's a margin problem, not a marketing problem"

The agent owns the customer relationship. Retailers become commodity fulfillment. Brand equity counts for nothing when a machine optimizes purely on price and reviews.

🎯 The Pragmatist — Benedict Evans

"Which tasks get automated — that's the question"

AI transitions happen slower than hype, faster than incumbents plan. The question isn't whether AI transforms commerce — it's which specific part of the journey it claims first.

The Expansionist View

"The past 30 years of digital evolution have shown a consistent pattern: new channels expand the ecosystem and are additive, rarely replacing what came before. Agentic commerce will follow the same trajectory, adding a powerful new layer of engagement, but not a wholesale replacement for existing ones."
Michael Komasinski, CEO, Criteo — "Agentic Commerce is Emerging, Just Not the Way Most People Expect" · December 16, 2025

This school of thought — backed by Criteo, Bain, and historical channel-shift data — argues that agentic commerce adds a powerful new engagement layer without demolishing existing ones. Today's LLM platforms are strongest when assisting with research and comparison, while shoppers still want to maintain control over final decisions.

The Structural Replacement View

"External LLM platforms will understand customer intent better than brands do. Brands will be invited into the decision process after intent is already formed. Visibility becomes conditional, paid, and reactive. This is not a marketing problem. It is a margin problem."
Medium · Cenk Karacaev, January 2026

The structural replacement camp argues prior channel shifts are insufficient analogies. Prior transitions (search → mobile → social) changed the channel but not the buyer. Agentic commerce changes the buyer from a human to a machine. Decisions are made before customers ever reach a brand-owned surface. Customers prompt. LLMs decide. That is a different order of disruption.

The Pragmatic Middle — Benedict Evans

"AI has become the new Metaverse, in that people say it and you have no idea what they mean. The most important question isn't whether AI replaces things — it's which specific tasks it automates, and where in the value chain that happens."
Benedict Evans, B2BMX 2026 Keynote

Evans placed AI in historical perspective alongside mainframes, PCs, and smartphones. The platform shift is real, but its specific value capture points are still being discovered. Transitions happen slower than hype suggests, faster than incumbents plan for, and never quite in the form anyone predicted.

The Retail Futurist View — Doug Stephens

Doug Stephens (Retail Prophet), whose frameworks have influenced Walmart, Google, and LVMH, argues the real question isn't whether channels die — it's whether retailers can articulate a reason for existing that transcends transaction. In an agent-mediated world, the retailers who survive will be those who provide something an agent cannot: judgment, community, trust, and human context.

The Economist's View

The Economist, October 2025

"AI search tools can rapidly process data from company websites and publicly available sources. This democratisation of information will make it very difficult for businesses to charge anything above the fairest price. Customer loyalty could shift from brands to the AI tools themselves."

Switching costs are falling. AI tools make price comparison so frictionless that years of brand investment can be neutralized in a single conversation. Scott Galloway's November 2025 Amazon analysis notes that investments in AI and robotics are beginning to deliver real operational leverage — with projected cost savings of $4 billion per year from next-generation fulfillment centers. The companies capturing the most agentic commerce value are those with logistics certainty, not just content quality.

Bull Case

Additive, Not Replacing

AI becomes a high-converting discovery channel. Sites persist for story and fulfillment. Specialty retailers with rich data get surfaced more, not less.

Bear Case

Structural Disintermediation

LLMs own the customer relationship. Retailers become fulfillment utilities. Brand equity is irrelevant to agents optimizing for price, speed, and ratings.

Most Likely

Uneven, Vertical-by-Vertical

The transition plays out differently by category. Low-consideration purchases go agentic fast. High-consideration purchases hybridize. The question is which category you're in.

The Unknown

Agent Preferences & Training

We don't yet know how agents will be monetized, what biases their training data introduces, or how brand mentions affect LLM recommendations.

Jenson USA operates in Zone 3 — where agents assist but humans decide. This is a defensible position, but only if product data is rich enough for agents to surface the right recommendations in the first place.

Bain's November 2025 consumer research found that around half of U.S. consumers remain cautious about letting AI handle an end-to-end transaction autonomously — and that adoption is expected to vary significantly by category. Low-consideration, recurring purchases are the natural first use case. For expensive, fit-dependent, or emotionally significant purchases, the question remains: would consumers really want an agent to decide for them?

Four Zones on the Spectrum

  • 1
    Full Delegation — Agents Win ImmediatelyReplenishment purchases, commodity goods, standardized products. Coffee pods, cleaning supplies, bulk consumables. The agent knows your preferences, monitors inventory, optimizes price. This zone is already moving fast.
  • 2
    Research Delegation, Human Decision — Current Sweet SpotElectronics, apparel accessories, general sporting goods. The agent does research, comparison, and shortlisting. The human makes the final call. This is where most agentic activity clusters right now and where AEO optimization matters most.
  • 3
    Hybrid — Agent Assists, Human CommitsBicycles, running shoes, ski equipment, furniture. High-consideration, fit-dependent, often emotionally significant. Agents can provide extraordinary research value. But the final purchase involves personal judgment agents will struggle to replace for years. This is Jenson's primary zone.
  • 4
    Human-Led, Agent-Resistant — Long-Term FortressLuxury goods, bespoke items, products requiring physical fitting. Agents may surface options, but the decision is owned by the human and often involves in-person evaluation. The store remains essential here.

The strategic implication: The more you shift your catalog and customer experience toward Zones 3 and 4 — where human judgment and domain expertise are irreplaceable — the more defensible your position in an agent-mediated world. Simultaneously, Zone 2 products must be machine-readable and agent-discoverable, or you lose them entirely.

The cycling category sits in Zone 3 almost by definition. A $3,500 carbon road bike involves geometry, fit, intended terrain, rider biomechanics, and component compatibility. An agent can do extraordinary research. But the trust required to fully delegate that decision will take years to develop — if it ever does for serious enthusiasts.

What LLMs Actually Need From Product Data

In answer engine environments, there is no list. There's one answer. LLMs need: entities they can recognize (e.g., "Specialized Roubaix Sport" is a product, "SRAM" is a brand) and facts they can verify (e.g., the reach is 383mm, the tire clearance is 38mm, the groupset is SRAM Rival AXS).

LLMs are essentially fact-extractors. A paragraph full of adjectives is useless to an AI. A paragraph with specific dimensions, weight limits, geometry specs, and performance ranges is gold. For cycling, every SKU needs: stack, reach, wheelbase, head tube angle, tire clearance, drivetrain compatibility, rider weight limit, intended use classification, and natural-language rider fit guidance. That level of data richness is rare. It is also a moat.

AEO — The New SEO

Real-world transaction data backs the strategic case. Alhena AI, a commerce AI platform vendor, published a 329-brand dataset showing LLM and generative AI traffic converting at 2.47% — above Google Ads (1.82%) and Meta Ads (0.52%). While Alhena has a commercial interest in agentic commerce adoption, the directional finding is consistent with Adobe Analytics and BCG data, which independently show AI-referred visitors converting better and engaging more deeply. Yet most ecommerce teams have zero strategy for getting their products recommended by AI. That gap is the opportunity right now.

GEO (Generative Engine Optimization), AEO (Answer Engine Optimization), LLMO, and ACO all refer to the same imperative: make your product data legible to machines that reason in language, not just index by keyword.

The Disintermediation Cascade

The most existential risk for multi-brand retailers is not just that traffic declines. It's that the entire customer relationship migrates to a platform that extracts value from every transaction while returning progressively less of it to the retailer.

The Three-Phase Disintermediation Cascade — Where Are We Now, and Where Is This Heading?
Phase 1 · NOW
Traffic & Attention Migration
Severity: Medium — but accelerating
Shoppers start their journey on ChatGPT or Perplexity instead of Google. They may still end up on your site to buy — but you're no longer the discovery destination. Organic search traffic begins declining. Your first-party behavioral data starts degrading because fewer shoppers visit your pages to browse.
📉 Organic traffic falls 👁 Less site discovery ⚠️ Browse data degrades
Phase 2 · 2026–2028
Transaction Migration
Severity: High
Checkout begins happening inside the LLM interface. OpenAI + PayPal enables purchase without visiting your site. Google's UCP protocol lets agents transact on behalf of users across 20+ partner retailers. The retailer is still fulfilling orders — but the customer experience is owned by the AI platform. You are now a fulfillment provider.
🛒 Checkout moves off your site 💳 LLM owns checkout data ⛔ You're a fulfillment service
Phase 3 · 2028+
Relationship Migration
Severity: Existential
The customer relationship belongs entirely to the LLM platform. They have the purchase history. They know preferences. Your loyalty program is irrelevant — the AI already knows more about the customer than your CRM does. Re-engagement costs rise permanently. The retailer without its own agent is a commodity supplier with no customer relationship at all.
💔 Loyalty programs irrelevant 🕳 CRM goes dark 📈 Re-acquisition costs surge forever
Most retailers are entering Phase 1 right now. The window to build the capabilities needed to avoid Phase 2 and 3 is the next 12–18 months. This is not a 5-year problem. It is a now problem.

Bain's Verdict: "Multibrand retailers and marketplaces will likely face a greater disintermediation threat. Third-party AI agents increase market transparency, giving big, low-cost, high-speed players an edge. Retailers that cannot compete on price, unique assortment, or shipping speed alone will need a competitive differentiator attractive to agents."

The Toll Bridge Owners

Amazon, Google, Walmart, and the LLM platforms are each building from very different starting positions. The LLM platforms are building toll bridges that want to own the road and become the destination. Amazon's response — Nova Act, an AI agent for web browsing and shopping — is its attempt to become the agent and the destination simultaneously. Most retailers have neither the scale nor the engineering capacity to attempt this.

The Martech & Customer Data Reckoning

For the past decade, retail marketing ran on: capture behavioral data → segment audiences → send personalized messages → re-engage at scale. That entire paradigm is under pressure.

In the old funnel, you had data at every step — browse behavior, cart additions, abandoned sessions, and conversion signals. In the new funnel, the agent owns the top of the funnel entirely. The behavioral data that powered email retargeting, personalization, and re-engagement simply doesn't exist anymore.

McKinsey warns that 20% to 50% of traffic from traditional search channels is at risk as AI-powered search displaces conventional search — framed as a risk scenario, not a central forecast. Marketers must now optimize for AI discovery, not just search engine rankings.

The First-Party Data Imperative

The central tension for any multi-brand retailer: agentic commerce reduces the data collection surface — fewer site visits, fewer browse events, fewer abandoned cart signals — precisely when first-party data has never been more commercially valuable. Retailers who invested in loyalty programs and first-party identity infrastructure before this shift have a meaningful structural advantage. Those who relied primarily on third-party cookie targeting and behavioral browse signals face the most acute data gap.

The Emerging "Brand Twin" Concept

One of the most interesting forward-looking concepts in martech is the "Brand Twin" — always-on, brand-owned AI agents that deeply understand individual consumers and act on their behalf, moving marketing from mass outreach toward relevance at scale. For retailers, the Brand Twin concept is more defensive than offensive: if you don't have an agent representing your catalog and customer relationships, a third-party LLM will — on terms you don't control.

Loyalty in an Agent World

There is a genuine risk that customer loyalty could shift from brands to the AI tools themselves. As AI tools make comparison frictionless, the 'leaving cost' is perceived as lower. What builds genuine loyalty in an agent world is: proprietary data the agent can use on your behalf, exclusive access or inventory, and service quality that exceeds what an agent can source elsewhere.

Beneath the strategic narrative is a rapidly consolidating technical infrastructure. Understanding the protocols being built right now is essential — these determine which retailers are agent-accessible and which are invisible.

Model Context Protocol (MCP)

MCP was introduced to solve a growing challenge in modern AI: how to allow models to interact securely and efficiently with external tools and data sources. Shopify rolled out built-in MCP support across all stores in its Summer 2025 Edition. Every Shopify store now exposes an MCP endpoint by default — an AI assistant can search the catalog, retrieve product details, add items to cart, answer FAQs, and initiate checkout, all in real time.

This is the infrastructure moment. The equivalent of deciding whether to integrate with Google Shopping feeds in 2012 — except the consequences of non-participation are more severe because agents don't return a list of results; they return one answer.

Universal Commerce Protocol (UCP)

Google's UCP, developed with Shopify, Etsy, Wayfair, Target, and Walmart, endorsed by 20+ global partners including Mastercard, Stripe, and Visa, is an open-source standard for agentic commerce. It establishes a common language for seamless commerce journeys between consumer surfaces, businesses, and payment providers, with tokenized payments and verifiable credentials.

Agent-to-Agent (A2A) Commerce

A2A empowers autonomous AI agents to coordinate, negotiate, and complete tasks directly with one another. The near-term implication: a shopper's personal agent negotiates with a retailer's agent for price, availability, and terms. The entire commercial interaction happens machine-to-machine — the human is never in the loop.

Strategic Implication

MCP + UCP + A2A together constitute the "plumbing" of agentic commerce. Retailers need to think of API infrastructure and data quality not as engineering concerns but as commercial real estate. If your catalog doesn't have an address in this ecosystem, your products don't exist to an agent.

The Vendor Landscape — Who's Building What

A generation of martech and commerce-technology vendors is being restructured around this shift. Some are pivoting successfully; others are being commoditized. Understanding the landscape helps identify where to invest and where to internalize.

Catalog Enrichment & Product Intelligence

Companies like Velou (AI-powered product tagging and attribute enrichment, with a strong Google Shopping feed focus) and an earlier generation of players like Lily AI are building on the insight that structured product data is the foundation of both search performance and LLM discoverability. If your data isn't clean, consistent, and attribute-rich at the SKU level, no amount of marketing spend will put you in front of an agent making a purchasing decision.

The build-vs-buy question is real: some of this enrichment work can be internalized with modern LLM tooling and strong editorial workflows, especially for specialty retailers with bounded catalog depth. The vendor opportunity is significant precisely because most retailer catalogs are in poor shape — missing attributes, inconsistent naming, empty spec fields, and marketing copy written for humans rather than machines.

Behavioral Trigger & Lifecycle Marketing Platforms

Platforms built on behavioral email and SMS personalization face a structural challenge as agentic commerce reduces the data collection surface. When agents browse on behalf of shoppers, the behavioral signals that powered trigger-based marketing disappear. The platforms adapting most effectively are pivoting toward first-party data activation, predictive modeling, and integration with LLM-native surfaces.

Search & Discovery Intelligence

Platforms focused on internal site search and faceted navigation are being directly threatened as agentic interfaces handle the discovery layer before a shopper ever reaches a retailer's site. The smart pivot is toward AEO tooling — helping retailers understand how their products appear in LLM recommendations. Enterprise AEO tools now provide multi-LLM simulation, testing identical queries across GPT-4o, Claude, and Gemini to reveal how different AI systems retrieve and weight your data.

The Three Threats — Stacked

Threat 1: Brand Bypass via Direct Agent Routing

When a consumer asks an agent "find me the best deal on a Trek Domane AL5 in size 56," the agent has no obligation to route through Jenson. If Trek, Specialized, or Shimano build their own MCP servers and agentic checkout flows — a near-certainty within 2–3 years — an agent optimizing for price and trust could route directly to the brand. Jenson's MAP pricing compliance and exclusive inventory become the primary defenses.

Threat 2: Catalog Data Disadvantage vs. Brands

Brands own their product data at the source. They know the geometry, the compatibility matrix, the intended use classification, the fit guidance. They can publish richer structured data than any multi-brand retailer working from vendor-supplied feeds. Jenson's catalog quality has to match or exceed the brand's own product pages — an extraordinary data investment, but a learnable discipline with LLM-assisted enrichment.

Threat 3: Transparency Commoditizes Price Position

Agents make price comparison essentially frictionless. The advantage a customer once got from knowing about Jenson's pricing — earned through discovery, loyalty, and repeat behavior — is now available to anyone with a single prompt. If Jenson's only differentiation is price, that advantage erodes rapidly. The response must be richer value: expertise, curation, service, and fit — things agents cannot fully replicate for years.

The Three Opportunities — Specific to Cycling

Opportunity 1: Become the Authority Hub for Cycling Intelligence

No general retailer, no LLM trained on broad web data, and no individual brand can be the most authoritative structured source on cycling across all categories. Jenson can. Bike fitting data, component compatibility matrices, groupset upgrade paths, geometry comparison tools, suspension setup guides, drivetrain service intervals — structured as machine-readable domain expertise, this becomes content LLMs cite, reference, and prefer. This is not a marketing investment. It is a data moat.

Opportunity 2: Encode Expert Judgment as Agent-Usable Data

Jenson's most defensible asset has always been cycling expertise — staff who ride, who fit, who have opinions. In an agent-mediated world, that expertise needs to be externalized as structured data: fit recommendations mapped to rider dimensions, component compatibility scores, terrain-specific build recommendations, upgrade path patterns. The Quovi widget architecture — where PDP conversations feed back into a knowledge base — is exactly the right direction.

Opportunity 3: Own the On-Site Agent Experience Before Off-Site Agents Do

According to Constructor.com's "Preparing Retail for Agentic AI" report, 43% of shoppers want an AI-powered search bar or chatbot to explain what they need in their own words. When asked about willingness to engage with a true "AI agent" on a retail site, 60% leaned positive — 23% would definitely try it and 37% probably would (only 13% said they'd likely refuse). An on-site agent that knows Jenson's full catalog, knows cycling deeply, and can guide a rider through a bike fit conversation is stronger differentiation than any banner campaign — and a data collection mechanism for every catalog gap.

What We Know With High Confidence

  • 1
    Discovery is already shifting, and it is acceleratingThe data from Adobe, Morgan Stanley, Criteo, and Bain is consistent: AI-driven traffic is growing exponentially, converting better than paid channels, and led by younger demographics who will be the dominant consumer cohort within a decade. This is not a future trend to plan for — it is a present trend to respond to.
  • 2
    Structured product data is now a commercial asset, not a technical detailAny retailer whose product data is not rich, consistent, and machine-readable is already losing LLM visibility to competitors who are. This is the most immediate, highest-leverage investment available. The cost of fixing it is high. The cost of not fixing it is higher.
  • 3
    The infrastructure layer is consolidating fastMCP is already deployed at scale on Shopify. UCP is backed by Google, Shopify, Visa, Mastercard, and 20+ global partners. These are not emerging standards — they are becoming the baseline. Retailers not integrated into this ecosystem within 18 months will face compounding disadvantage.
  • 4
    First-party data becomes more valuable as behavioral signal degradesThe more commerce moves through agent interfaces, the less behavioral data retailers capture from site sessions. Retailers who have built deep first-party data assets — purchase history, preference signals, fit data — have a structural advantage that will appreciate in value.

What We Don't Know — The Honest Uncertainties

The pace of full transaction delegation remains genuinely uncertain for high-consideration categories. Around half of consumers say they are not ready to let AI handle an end-to-end transaction without their oversight. That number will change — but how fast, and for which categories, is not yet clear.

The monetization model for LLM platforms in commerce is unresolved. If platforms monetize through advertising, the dynamics resemble Google Shopping. If through transaction fees, they resemble Amazon Marketplace. If they remain neutral citation engines, data quality wins. Different monetization structures have profoundly different implications for retailer strategy.

Finally: we don't know how agent preferences are shaped. An agent trained primarily on Amazon data will have different product knowledge biases than one trained on a broader web corpus. The training data embedded in LLMs is not transparent, and it may systematically advantage some retailers and brands in ways that have nothing to do with product quality or data structure.

The Single Most Important Conclusion

Core Strategic Principle

The retailers that survive agentic commerce won't be the ones with the most SKUs, the most ad spend, or even the best prices. They will be the ones whose expertise, knowledge, and product intelligence the agent trusts — because they have encoded that intelligence as structured data, published it as authoritative content, and built the technical infrastructure for agents to access it reliably. In specialty retail, expertise has always been the moat. In agentic commerce, that expertise needs to live in a format machines can reason over. The window to build it before competitors do is closing. For a vertical like cycling — where the knowledge requirements are genuinely complex and general platforms are genuinely thin — the opportunity to own that knowledge layer is real, defensible, and worth building now.

History suggests that resisting new intermediaries rarely works. Retailers who refused to list on Amazon lost reach; those who saw mobile as a distraction fell behind. Agents aren't adversaries — they're channels. The sooner retailers treat them as distribution, the sooner they can shape how their products are represented.

The website is not dead. The product page is not going away. But its role is changing: from being the destination of the shopping journey, it is becoming the data source that feeds an ecosystem of agents, assistants, and interfaces that will surface products to buyers who may never visit it at all. Design accordingly.
